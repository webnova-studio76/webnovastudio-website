IaaS, PaaS, and SaaS

The "Pizza as a Service" analogy is the best way to understand cloud models. Do you buy the ingredients (IaaS), get it delivered (PaaS), or eat at the restaurant (SaaS)?

IaaS

Infrastructure as a Service. You rent the raw hardware (Servers, Storage, Networking). You manage the OS and Apps.
Ex: AWS EC2, Azure VMs.

PaaS

Platform as a Service. Provides a framework for developers to build apps without worrying about servers.
Ex: Google App Engine, Heroku.

SaaS

Software as a Service. Completed applications accessed via a browser.
Ex: Gmail, Office 365, Salesforce.

Deployment Architectures

Where is the cloud located and who has access to it?

  • Public Cloud: Services are offered over the public internet and owned by a provider (AWS, Google). High scalability, low cost.
  • Private Cloud: Used exclusively by one organization. Can be physically located at the company's data center. High security and control.
  • Hybrid Cloud: A mix of both. Sensitive data stays on the Private cloud, while high-traffic apps run on the Public cloud.
Multi-Cloud: Many modern companies use multiple providers (AWS + Azure) to ensure that if one provider goes down, their business stays online.

Cloud Economics & Responsibility

1. Capex vs. Opex

The Cloud shifts your spending from Capital Expenditure (buying expensive servers upfront) to Operating Expenditure (paying a monthly bill for only what you use).

2. The Shared Responsibility Model

A critical security concept. The Cloud Provider is responsible for the Security OF the Cloud (hardware, cables), while the Customer is responsible for Security IN the Cloud (their data, passwords, and apps).

Elasticity vs. Scalability:
Scalability is the ability to handle more load.
Elasticity is the ability to automatically shrink back down when the load is gone to save money.
