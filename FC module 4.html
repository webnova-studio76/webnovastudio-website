Types of Networks (Scope)

Networks are classified based on the geographical area they cover.

PAN (Personal)

Range: ~10 meters. Used for personal devices (Bluetooth headphones to a phone).

LAN (Local)

Range: A building or office. High speed and private (Your home Wi-Fi).

MAN (Metropolitan)

Range: A city. Connects multiple LANs across a town (Cable TV network).

WAN (Wide)

Range: Countries or Continents. The Internet is the world's largest WAN.

Network Topologies

The geometric arrangement of how computers are connected.

  • Star Topology: All nodes connect to a central Hub/Switch. (Most common today).
  • Bus Topology: All nodes share a single main cable.
  • Ring Topology: Nodes are connected in a closed loop.
  • Mesh Topology: Every node is connected to every other node. (High redundancy).
Star Power: If one cable fails in a Star network, only that computer goes down. In a Bus network, the entire network can fail!

Networking Hardware

The physical devices that make communication possible.

Device Function
NIC Network Interface Card. Connects your PC to the network.
Switch Connects devices on a LAN and sends data only to the target device.
Router The "Traffic Police." Connects different networks (e.g., your Home to the Internet).
Modem Modulates signals from your ISP into digital data for your router.

The Language of the Web

Protocols are sets of rules that govern how data is transmitted.

1. IP (Internet Protocol)

Every device has a unique address. Think of it as a digital "Home Address."

  • IPv4: 32-bit (e.g., 192.168.1.1)
  • IPv6: 128-bit (e.g., 2001:0db8:85a3...) - Created because we ran out of IPv4 addresses.

2. DNS (Domain Name System)

The "Phonebook of the Internet." It translates human names (google.com) into IP addresses (142.250.190.46).

3. HTTP/HTTPS

The protocol for transferring web pages. The 'S' stands for Secure (Encryption), which is mandatory for modern websites.
